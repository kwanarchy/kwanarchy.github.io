Home > [Reader Submission] Capitalism: The Mother of Artificial Scarcity

Apr 11, 2021

Tags: Reader Submission, Artificial Scarcity, Liberalism, Rightwing

We have often been told that capitalism and the free market system are the greatest ways to run society off of. Both Neoliberals and the rightwing both believe that having a system with no control or “mind of it’s own” is the best way to handle the allocation of resources and services through out society. But is this true?

There is no question that the free market system has its benefits, however one BIG and often overlooked consequence is Artificial Resource Scarcity.

What is artificial resource scarcity? In simplest terms, it is the man made shortage of available resources (i.e; food, building materials, water, etc) in an environment rich with these resources. Simply put, just because people are surrounded by resources, does not mean they are able to access them economically.

Go to a grocery store dumpster and you will find tons of edible and safe to eat vegetables and produce which have been discarded. Go to a construction site dumpster and you will find tons of useful materials destined to go to the landfill while the price of lumber skyrockets. This “artificial” scarcity is a man-made issue.

Furthermore, the “free-market” system is not really “free”. It is common practice for governments to pay farmers to destroy large quantities of their crops in order to effectively manipulate the supply and demand mechanism, which raises prices up. While this may be good for a few benefactors, it jacks up the price of food significantly, just so farmers can make a few more bucks off of working class families. This shows the “free-market” system and capitalism are just mechanisms to inflate the cost of living and deplete the planet of precious resources through wasteful practices.

We as a society clearly need a new system, a system which does not depend on intentionally fabricated shortages of goods or people going without their needs met in order to function.

-Anonymous
